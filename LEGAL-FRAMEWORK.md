# THE LEGAL FRAMEWORK — How the Markup is Legal

## Six Interlocking Laws Create a Mandatory Two-Layer Billing Structure

The markup between what the state pays ($40.72/hr) and what the aide earns ($18.50/hr) is not fraud. It's architecture — built by six laws that interlock to create a system where money must pass through two organizations before reaching the worker.

---

## LAW 1: MGL Chapter 19A, Section 4B — The Mandatory Middleman

**What it does:** Creates "Aging Services Access Points" (ASAPs) as the sole gateway to elder home care. ASAPs assess elders, develop care plans, and purchase services.

**The critical clause:**

> "ASAPs shall not provide direct services except for case management; information and referral, and protective services."
> — MGL c. 19A, § 4B

**What this means:** Mystic Valley Elder Services is **legally prohibited** from employing the home health aides. The law forces them to subcontract. The 72% pass-through to for-profit LLCs isn't a business choice — it's a statutory requirement.

**Source:** malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleII/Chapter19A/Section4B

---

## LAW 2: MGL Chapter 118E, Section 13D — The Rate-Setting Authority

**What it does:** Gives the Secretary of EOHHS sole authority to set Medicaid reimbursement rates for social service programs.

**The problem:** The State Auditor found that EOHHS has **no documented policies or procedures** for how it actually calculates rates:

> "EOHHS has not established policies and procedures documenting how rates are developed for human-service providers' contracts under Chapter 257. As a result, there is no assurance that a consistent method is used to develop the rates paid to human-service providers."
> — MA State Auditor, Review of Chapter 257 Implementation

**What this means:** The G0156 rate of $10.18/unit was set by EOHHS under this authority, but the methodology for arriving at that number is not publicly documented.

**Source:** mass.gov/audit/review-of-certain-aspects-of-the-implementation-of-chapter-257-of-the-acts-of-2008

---

## LAW 3: Chapter 257 of the Acts of 2008 — Cost-to-Rate Conversion

**What it does:** Requires EOHHS to set rates for human service providers based on their actual reported costs, reviewed every two years.

**The cycle:**
1. Union contracts raise worker wages
2. Provider labor costs increase
3. Providers report higher costs to the state
4. Chapter 257 formula converts actual costs into Medicaid rates
5. Providers bill the new higher rates
6. State Auditor found providers accumulated **surplus revenues with minimal wage increases to frontline workers**
7. Providers' Council and ADDP lobby for the next rate increase
8. Repeat

**Key facts:**
- Requires 75% wage pass-through — but **no tracking mechanism exists**
- Providers' Council (trade association) successfully sued EOHHS for not meeting rate-setting deadlines
- $800M+ provider reserve fund accumulated
- ADDP (chaired by Seven Hills Foundation CEO, $797K comp) successfully sued for higher rates

**What this means:** Rates are based on what providers say they spend, not on benchmarks for efficiency. The more they spend, the higher the rates go. There is no cap.

**Source:** malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2008/Chapter257

---

## LAW 4: 101 CMR 350.04 — The Specific Rate Regulation

**What it does:** Sets the G0156 home health aide rate at $10.18 per 15-minute unit ($40.72/hour).

**Key language:**

> "The allowable fees are full compensation for the home health services rendered including, but not limited to, administrative or supervisory duties and costs in connection with service provision."
> — 101 CMR 350.01(3)

**What this means:** The $40.72/hr is supposed to cover everything — the aide's wage AND all overhead. There is no separate administrative fee. Yet the money flows through two organizations (ASAP + LLC), each taking a cut, before reaching the aide.

**Source:** mass.gov/regulations/101-CMR-350.00 | law.cornell.edu/regulations/massachusetts/101-CMR-350-04

---

## LAW 5: 957 CMR 6.00 — Cost Reporting Requirements

**What it does:** Requires home health agencies receiving MassHealth revenue >$50K to file annual Nursing Services Cost Reports with CHIA.

**What this means:** The for-profit LLCs (Greater Boston Patient Care, Connected Home Care, Global Healthcare Services) that receive the 72% pass-through ARE required to report their costs to the state. These reports break down direct care wages, admin costs, overhead, and profit.

**The gap:** These cost reports are filed with CHIA but are not proactively published. They're available only via public records request (email: public.records@chiamass.gov, first 4 hours free).

**This is the last piece of the proof chain.** Filing a PRR for the Nursing Services Cost Reports of MVES's top subcontractors would reveal exactly what the LLCs pay their aides and keep as overhead.

**Source:** chiamass.gov/nursing-services-cost-reports-2

## LAW 6: Federal — 42 U.S.C. § 1396a (Medicaid Act) + Older Americans Act

**What it does:**
- Medicaid Act requires states to set provider payment rates "sufficient to enlist enough providers" — but gives states wide discretion on how much to pay
- Federal match is ~65% for MA, meaning 65 cents of every Medicaid dollar comes from federal taxpayers, not just Massachusetts
- Older Americans Act (42 U.S.C. 3021 et seq.) funds the Area Agency on Aging structure nationally

**What this means:** Massachusetts chose to layer the federal AAA structure with state-specific ASAP requirements (Law 1) and its own rate-setting methodology (Laws 2-4). The federal government matches whatever Massachusetts decides to pay — so the 120% markup is subsidized 65% by federal taxpayers.

## HOW THE LAWS INTERLOCK

```
FEDERAL: Medicaid Act + OAA
  → Provides 65% match for whatever MA decides to pay
  → Creates Area Agency on Aging structure

MGL Ch. 19A § 4B (Law 1)
  → Creates ASAPs as mandatory middlemen
  → PROHIBITS ASAPs from employing aides directly
  → Forces subcontracting to home care agencies

MGL Ch. 118E § 13D + Chapter 257 (Laws 2-3)
  → EOHHS Secretary sets rates based on provider costs
  → No documented methodology (per State Auditor)
  → No cap on rate increases
  → 75% wage pass-through required but NOT tracked

101 CMR 350.04 (Law 4)
  → Sets G0156 at $10.18/unit = $40.72/hr
  → Rate is "full compensation" including admin
  → Yet money passes through two orgs before reaching worker

957 CMR 6.00 (Law 5)
  → LLCs must report costs to CHIA
  → Reports not published — available only via PRR
  → This is the accountability mechanism, but it's passive
```

**The result:** A legally mandated two-layer billing structure where:
1. The entity receiving state money (ASAP) is prohibited from employing the worker
2. The rate is set based on provider costs with no cap or efficiency benchmark
3. The rate-setting process has no documented methodology
4. The required wage pass-through has no tracking mechanism
5. The subcontractors' cost data exists but isn't proactively published

**None of it is illegal. That's the point.** The legislature built it this way, and the entities in this investigation lobbied for every piece of it.
